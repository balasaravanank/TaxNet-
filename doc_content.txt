[Heading 1] 1. GST — From Zero
[Heading 2] 1.1 What is GST?
[Body] GST stands for Goods and Services Tax. It is a single, unified tax that India applies to almost every product and service sold in the country. Before GST, India had dozens of different taxes — sales tax, service tax, excise duty, VAT — all layered on top of each other, confusing everyone.
[Body] GST replaced all of those with one clean tax system. It was introduced on 1 July 2017.
[Heading 2] 1.2 How GST Works — A Simple Example
[Body] Imagine a supply chain: Raw Material Supplier → Manufacturer → Retailer → Customer.
[Heading 2] 1.3 What is Input Tax Credit (ITC)? — The Key Concept
[Body] ITC (Input Tax Credit) is the hero and villain of GST. It is the mechanism that prevents double taxation — every business can claim back the GST it already paid to its supplier.
[Body] This is exactly what fraudsters exploit. If you can fake an invoice showing you 'paid' GST to a supplier, you can claim a refund of that GST without ever actually paying it.
[Heading 2] 1.4 What is a GSTIN?
[Body] Every business registered under GST gets a unique 15-digit ID called a GSTIN (GST Identification Number). Think of it like a business's PAN card, but for GST purposes.

[Heading 2] 1.6 How GST Fraud Happens — The 3 Main Types
[Body] Type 1: Fake Invoice Fraud (Most Common)
[Body] A fraudster creates a fake company (called a 'shell company' or 'ghost firm') that exists only on paper. This shell company issues fake invoices to real businesses — claiming it sold goods or services to them. The real business uses these fake invoices to claim ITC (get a tax refund) even though no goods were ever bought or sold. The shell company disappears before the tax authorities notice.
[Body] Type 2: Circular Trading / Carousel Fraud
[Body] This is the sophisticated version. A group of companies (say Company A, B, C, D) create a circle of fake invoices: A invoices B, B invoices C, C invoices D, D invoices A. Each company claims ITC from the previous invoice. The GST 'paid' just circulates in a loop — but everyone is claiming refunds on it. No real goods move. Only fake invoices do.
[Body] Type 3: Excess ITC Claims
[Body] A legitimate business inflates its purchase invoices — or buys invoices from fake suppliers — to claim more ITC than it is entitled to. The tax mismatch (ITC claimed > tax actually paid by supplier) is the telltale sign.

[Heading 1] 3. Core Technology Concepts — What They Are and Why They Matter
[Heading 2] 3.1 What is a Graph? (Graph Theory Basics)
[Body] In computer science, a 'graph' is NOT a bar chart or pie chart. A graph is a collection of NODES (dots) connected by EDGES (lines).
[Body] Why is a graph perfect for this problem? Because circular fraud is literally a cycle in a graph. You cannot see a cycle by looking at rows in a spreadsheet — you need to BUILD the graph and then TRAVERSE it to find cycles.
[Heading 2] 3.2 What is DFS? (Depth First Search)
[Body] DFS (Depth First Search) is an algorithm for exploring a graph. It starts at a node, goes as deep as possible along one path, then backtracks and tries another path. DFS is perfect for detecting cycles.
[Heading 2] 3.3 What is Machine Learning? (ML Basics)
[Body] Machine Learning is a type of AI where you train a computer program to recognize patterns by showing it lots of examples, rather than writing explicit rules. The program learns the patterns by itself.
[Body] In fraud detection, you feed the ML model data about normal companies (how much tax they pay, how many invoices they raise, etc.) and it learns what 'normal' looks like. Then when it sees a company that looks very different from normal, it flags it as suspicious (an anomaly).
[Body] Isolation Forest — What it does
[Body] Isolation Forest is an ML algorithm specifically designed to find anomalies. The idea: normal data points are hard to isolate because they are surrounded by many similar points. Anomalies are easy to isolate because they stand alone.
[Body] The algorithm builds random 'decision trees' that randomly split data. Data points that get isolated quickly (near the top of the tree) are anomalies. The algorithm gives each entity a score between -1 (definitely anomalous) and +1 (definitely normal).
[Body] Z-Score — What it does
[Body] Z-Score is a simple statistical measure that tells you how far a data point is from the average, measured in units called 'standard deviations'. A Z-Score of 0 means exactly average. A Z-Score of 3 means the value is 3 standard deviations above average — which is highly unusual.
[Heading 2] 3.4 What is RAG? (Retrieval-Augmented Generation)
[Body] RAG stands for Retrieval-Augmented Generation. It is a technique that makes AI answers smarter by first RETRIEVING relevant information from a knowledge base, then using that retrieved context to GENERATE a better answer.
[Body] Without RAG: You ask an AI 'Why is Company X suspicious?' The AI answers from general knowledge — vague and unhelpful.
[Body] With RAG: You ask 'Why is Company X suspicious?' The system first retrieves Company X's actual filing data, the specific GST rule being violated, and past similar fraud case summaries — then generates a precise, evidence-backed answer.
[Heading 2] 3.5 What is a Vector Database?
[Body] A Vector Database (like ChromaDB, which you use) stores data as mathematical vectors — lists of numbers that represent the 'meaning' of text. Similar texts have vectors that are mathematically close to each other.
[Body] When you search for 'circular trading fraud involving ITC mismatch', the vector database finds the document chunks whose meaning is most similar to your query — even if they don't use the exact same words. This is called 'semantic search'.
[Heading 2] 3.6 What is an LLM?
[Body] LLM stands for Large Language Model. It is a powerful AI model trained on enormous amounts of text that can understand and generate human language. Examples include Claude (by Anthropic), GPT-4 (by OpenAI), and Gemini (by Google).
[Body] In your system, the LLM receives: (a) the fraud signals detected by your graph + ML system, (b) the relevant GST rules retrieved by the RAG system — and generates a plain-English investigation summary for the auditor.

[Heading 2] 6.3 The Embedding Process
[Body] Before the system can search the knowledge base, all documents must be converted to vector embeddings. This happens once at startup (via a seed_vectorstore.py script).
[List Paragraph] Load all knowledge base documents (PDFs, text files, scraped web pages)
[List Paragraph] Chunk them into smaller pieces (~500 words each) — so retrieval is precise, not the whole document
[List Paragraph] For each chunk, run it through an embedding model (e.g., sentence-transformers/all-MiniLM-L6-v2 — free, runs locally)
[List Paragraph] The embedding model converts the text chunk into a vector — a list of ~384 numbers representing the meaning
[List Paragraph] Store the vector + the original text in ChromaDB
[Body] Example: The phrase 'circular trading invoice fraud' becomes a vector like [0.23, -0.45, 0.87, ...] (384 numbers). Similar meanings produce similar vectors.
[Heading 2] 6.4 The Retrieval Process (at query time)
[List Paragraph] Auditor clicks on Entity X. The system builds a query: 'Entity GSTIN 29XYZ has circular trading ring with 4 companies, ITC mismatch Rs. 30L, anomaly score 89/100'
[List Paragraph] This query is also embedded into a vector using the same embedding model
[List Paragraph] ChromaDB does a similarity search — finds the top 5 knowledge base chunks whose vectors are most similar to the query vector
[List Paragraph] Those 5 chunks (relevant GST rules, past fraud summaries) are returned as context
[List Paragraph] LangChain assembles the final prompt: query + context + instruction → sends to LLM API
[List Paragraph] LLM generates a precise, evidence-backed investigation summary in 3–5 sentences

[TABLE] Step-by-step GST flow (example: 18% GST on a Rs. 1000 product)
Step 1 — Supplier sells raw materials to Manufacturer for Rs. 500. Charges 18% GST = Rs. 90. Collects Rs. 590 total.
Step 2 — Manufacturer processes the goods, sells final product to Retailer for Rs. 800. Charges 18% GST = Rs. 144.
         BUT Manufacturer already paid Rs. 90 GST to the Supplier (this is called Input Tax Credit / ITC).
         So Manufacturer pays only Rs. 144 - Rs. 90 = Rs. 54 to the government. Net tax = Rs. 54.
Step 3 — Retailer sells to Customer for Rs. 1000. Charges 18% GST = Rs. 180.
         Retailer already paid Rs. 144 GST to Manufacturer (ITC). Pays only Rs. 180 - Rs. 144 = Rs. 36.
Final — Customer pays Rs. 1180 total. Government receives Rs. 90 + Rs. 54 + Rs. 36 = Rs. 180. The math works perfectly.

[TABLE] PART 3 — TECH CONCEPTS EXPLAINED FROM SCRATCH
[TABLE] Graph in the context of GST fraud
NODE    = A company (identified by its GSTIN)
EDGE    = An invoice between two companies (an arrow from seller → buyer)
WEIGHT  = The invoice amount

Example: If Company A sells to Company B, there is a directed edge from A → B.
If B sells to C, there is an edge from B → C.
If C sells BACK to A, there is an edge from C → A.
This creates a CYCLE: A → B → C → A — that is your circular trading fraud ring!
[TABLE] How DFS detects a fraud ring (simplified)
1. Start at Company A. Mark it as 'visited'.
2. Follow the edge to Company B. Mark B as visited.
3. Follow the edge to Company C. Mark C as visited.
4. Follow the edge from C → A. But A is already marked as 'visited'!
5. CYCLE DETECTED! A → B → C → A is a circular trading ring.
   Alert the auditor: 'These 3 companies are involved in circular invoicing.'
[TABLE] Z-Score example in GST context
Average ITC claimed by similar companies: Rs. 5 Lakh/month
Standard deviation (spread): Rs. 1 Lakh
Company X claims: Rs. 12 Lakh/month
Z-Score = (12 - 5) / 1 = 7.0  →  This is extremely suspicious!
Normal range: Z-Score between -2 and +2. Above ±3 is flagged.
